## Aisha's Saturday Afternoon

It is Saturday afternoon. Aisha is curled up on the couch with a tablet in her hands. She started by watching one short cooking video. That was about an hour ago. Maybe two hours. She isn't sure.

Her stomach rumbles. The sun outside has moved across the floor. Her best friend texted her about going to the park, but Aisha didn't notice the message until just now. Her eyes feel a little dry, and she feels grumpy in a way she can't quite explain.

Has that ever happened to you? You sit down for one quick video and look up and the day is almost gone. This chapter is about why that happens, what your body and brain are trying to tell you, and the simple habits that can help you stay in charge of your screens.

## Three Kinds of Online Activities

In Chapter 1 you learned that an *online activity* is anything you do on a digital device that uses the internet. But not all online activities are the same. Some make you feel close to people. Some make you think hard. Some get your body moving. Helpers and teachers around the world use a simple way to sort them into three groups.

A **heart activity** is an online activity that connects you to people you love or care about. Video-calling your grandma who lives far away is a heart activity. Sending a happy birthday message to a cousin is a heart activity. Heart activities are about *people*.

A **brain activity** is an online activity that helps you learn, think, create, or solve a problem. Looking up how a volcano works is a brain activity. Doing math practice on a school website is a brain activity. Writing a story or making a drawing on a tablet is a brain activity. Brain activities are about *learning and creating*.

A **body activity** is an online activity that gets your body moving. Following along with a kid yoga video is a body activity. Dancing to a music video in your living room is a body activity. Body activities are about *moving*.

A great day with screens has at least one of each. Look at the difference:

| Heart | Brain | Body |
|---|---|---|
| Video call with a cousin | Researching your science project | Following a dance-along video |
| Sending a kind message to a friend | Reading an online story | A "stretch with me" workout |
| Sharing a drawing with your aunt | A math practice game | A scavenger hunt that gets you outside |

When you mix all three, your screen time feels good and your day feels full. When you only do one kind for hours and hours, your day starts to feel like Aisha's — a little flat, a little grumpy, a little lost.

## Active Use and Passive Use

There is one more way to think about online activities. Some of them make you do things. Others just wash over you while you sit still.

**Active vs passive use** is the difference between making choices on a screen and letting the screen choose for you. *Active* use means you are typing, drawing, talking, asking, or creating. *Passive* use means you are just watching whatever shows up next. Reading a book to your little brother over a video call is active use. Watching one auto-play video after another for an hour is passive use.

Active use isn't always "good" and passive use isn't always "bad." Watching one funny video to relax is fine. The trouble starts when passive use takes over the whole afternoon. There is even a name for the kind of passive use that makes you feel worst.

**Doomscrolling** is when you keep swiping through video after video or post after post without choosing to stop, even though it isn't making you happy. Your thumb keeps moving. Your face stops smiling. You feel a little worse with every swipe. That is doomscrolling, and it can sneak up on anybody — even grown-ups.

## Notifications and Screen Time

Why is it so hard to stop sometimes? Part of the answer is that your devices are designed to *call you back*. They use little alerts called notifications.

A **notification** is a small alert from an app that pops up to grab your attention. It might be a sound, a buzz, a red dot, or a bubble of words on the screen. A notification might tell you that a friend sent a message, or that a new video is ready, or that a game wants you to come back and play.

Notifications can be helpful. A notification reminding you about your soccer practice is helpful. A notification telling you that a stranger is at the front door is helpful. But many notifications are not helpful — they just want you to pick up the device again. Every time you do, your screen time goes up a little.

**Screen time** is the total amount of time you spend looking at a screen in a day. Most tablets, phones, and laptops keep track of your screen time automatically. You can ask a trusted adult to help you find the screen time number on the device you use most. The number may surprise you. Aisha's tablet said *four hours and twelve minutes* by the time she looked up from her cooking videos.

Screen time is not a grade. It is not something to feel bad about. It is just a piece of information — like checking how much water you drank today. Knowing the number is the first step to making a choice about it.

## Signs Your Tech Is Out of Balance

Your body and your brain are very smart. They send you little signals when something is off. The trick is to *notice* the signals. Here are some of the most common ones.

A **grumpy mood** is when you feel cranky, snappy, or sad for no clear reason. After a long stretch of passive screen time, a grumpy mood often shows up. Your shoulders tighten. You feel annoyed at small things. You snap at the people you love.

A **skipped meal** happens when you are so caught up in a screen that you forget to eat. Aisha's rumbling stomach was a skipped-meal warning. Your body needs food, even when your eyes are glued to a video.

**Skipped play** happens when screen time pushes out the time you would have spent playing outside, riding a bike, building something, or just running around with friends. Your friend's text about the park was a skipped-play warning for Aisha.

**Tired eyes** are eyes that feel sore, dry, blurry, or scratchy after staring at a screen for too long. The medical name for this feeling is eye strain.

**Eye strain** is the doctor word for tired eyes from looking at a screen too long without a break. It is not dangerous, but it is your eyes telling you to look away. A common rule that eye doctors teach is the *20-20-20 rule*: every 20 minutes, look at something about 20 feet away for at least 20 seconds.

When your tech use is balanced, those four warning signs stay quiet. We have a name for that good feeling.

**Media balance** is the healthy mix of screen time and not-screen time that helps your heart, brain, and body all feel good. A balanced day might have one heart activity, one brain activity, one body activity, and a lot of time *off* screens — eating, playing, reading a paper book, talking with family, sleeping.

When the mix tips too far toward screens, balance becomes imbalance.

**Media imbalance** is when screens take over so much of your day that other parts of your life suffer. The four warning signs all show up at once: grumpy mood, skipped meals, skipped play, tired eyes. Aisha's afternoon was a media imbalance.

Here are the warning signs in one quick list, so they are easy to remember:

- Grumpy mood for no clear reason
- A skipped meal — you forgot to eat
- Skipped play — you missed time with friends or outside
- Tired eyes that feel dry, sore, or blurry
- A "where did the time go?" feeling when you finally look up

If you notice even one of these signs, that is a good moment to put the screen down and do something else. If you notice three or four at the same time, your body is shouting at you. Listen to it.

If a media imbalance is making you feel really bad, or if you can't seem to put the screen down even when you want to, tell a trusted adult. They will not be angry. They want to help you find a better balance, and they have probably had the same feeling themselves. You will not be in trouble for telling.

## Activity Sorting — A Habit You Can Use Today

Knowing the words is the first step. The second step is a habit you can practice. We call it activity sorting.

**Activity sorting** is the habit of stopping for one second before or during a screen activity and asking yourself two quick questions: *Is this a heart, brain, or body activity? Am I being active or passive?* That tiny pause helps you see what your screen time is really doing for you.

Here are the two questions in order:

1. **Heart, brain, or body?** — Which kind of online activity is this?
2. **Active or passive?** — Am I making choices and creating, or am I just watching?

If your answer is "brain and active" — like working on a story you are writing — that's a great use of screen time. If your answer is "I don't know, I'm just kind of swiping" — that is your sign to pause, think, and choose what to do next on purpose.

## Aisha's Saturday, Take Two

Aisha puts the tablet down on the couch and stands up. Her legs feel stiff. She walks to the kitchen and eats an apple. She texts her friend back about the park. She splashes some cool water on her face, and her tired eyes feel a little better right away.

Then she picks up the tablet again — but this time on purpose. She opens a video call to her cousin and they draw silly pictures together for fifteen minutes. That is a heart activity, and it is active use. After they hang up, Aisha grabs her bike and meets her friend at the park.

The whole day didn't disappear. Aisha just *noticed* what was happening, and she chose differently. That is media balance, and you can do it too — every single day.
